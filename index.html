Dr. Carl Sagan

The man who inspired a generation of astrophysicists - seen on television by over 500 million people in 60 different countires.

A Brief Timeline of Carl Sagan's Life

  • 1934 - Carl Sagan was born in Brooklyn, New York. His father was an immigrant garment worker from present day Ukraine.
  • 1947 - The year that inaugurated the "flying saucer" mass hysteria. Carl suspected the "discs" might be alien spaceships.
  • 1949 - After moving to New Jersey, Carl graduated High School in 1951 as the president of the school's chemistry club.
  • 1954 - He graduated with honors from the University of Chicago, one of the few universities to admit a 16-year-old.
  • 1956 - Carl received an M.S. degree in physics, and eventually earned a Ph.D. in 1960. His thesis: The Physical Studies of Planets.
  • 1958 - He worked on classified milatary Project A119, the secret Air Force plan to detonate a nuclear warhead on the Moon.
  • 1960 - After college he became a fellow at the University of California, Berkeley and also worked with NASA's Mariner 2 team.
  • 1968 - He was denied tenure at Harvard, having been seen as someone that focused too broadly across different areas of science.
  • 1972 - Carl designed the first physical messages ever sent into space: a gold-plated plaque, attached to space probe Pioneer 10.
  • 1976 - He taught at Cornell, receiving numerous promotions. Cornell welcomed his growing celebrity status, unlike Harvard.
  • 1980 - Cosmos: A Personal Voyage first aired. It became the most widely watched series in the entire history of American public television.
  • 1985 - He authored the science fiction novel Contact, the basis for the 1997 film.
  • 1989 - During an interview with Ted Turner, Carl was facetiously asked if he believed in socialism. "I'm not sure what a socialist is. But, I believe the government has a responsibility to care for the people." he responded.
  • 1996 - After suffering from myelodysplasia for two years and receiving three bone marrow transplants from his sister, Sagan died from pneumonia at the age of 62.
