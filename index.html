Carl Sagan

“We're made of star stuff. We are a way for the cosmos to know itself.”

A Brief Timeline of Carl Sagan’s Life

  • 1934 - Carl Sagan was born in Brooklyn, New York. His father was an immigrant garment worker. His mother a housewife.
  • 1947 - The year that inaugurated the "flying saucer" craze, Carl Sagan as a child suspected the "discs" might be alien spaceships.
  • 1949 - His family moved to the Rahway, New Jersey. He graduated High School in 1951, and was the president of the school's chemistry club.
  • 1954 - He graduated with honors from the University of Chicago, one of the few universities to admit a 16-year-old.
  • 1956 - He earned a M.S. degree in physics, eventually earning a Ph.D. degree in 1960 with his thesis Physical Studies of Planets
  • 1958 - He worked on classified military Project A119, the secret Air Force plan to detonate a nuclear warhead on the Moon.
  • 1960 - Sagan was a Miller Fellow at the University of California, Berkeley and also worked with NASA's Mariner 2 team.
  • 1968 - Sagan was denied tenure at Harvard. The tenure denial has been blamed on several factors, including that he focused his interests too broadly across a number of areas.
  • 1972 - Sagan assembled the first physical messages sent into space: the Pioneer plaque and the Voyager Golden Record, universal messages that could potentially be understood by any extraterrestrial intelligence that might find them.
  • 1976 - Held the position of David Duncan Professor of Astronomy and Space Sciences at Cornel (till his passing).
  • 1980 - Narrated and co-wrote the award-winning 1980 television series Cosmos: A Personal Voyage. The most widely watched series in the history of American public television, Cosmos has been seen by at least 500 million people across 60 different countries.
  • 1985 - Published the science fiction novel Contact, the basis for a 1997 film of the same name.
  • 1989 - Interviewed by Ted Turner. When asked whether he believed in socialism he responded: "I'm not sure what a socialist is. But I believe the government has a responsibility to care for the people... making the people self-reliant."
  • 1996 - After suffering from myelodysplasia for two years and receiving three bone marrow transplants from his sister, Sagan died from pneumonia at the age of 62.
